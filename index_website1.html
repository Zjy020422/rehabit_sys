Why We Built This

We were ski teammates at college. Both of us tore our ACLs within six months of each other. During recovery, we spent a lot of time comparing notes‚Äîphysical therapy bills, how many sessions we could actually afford, and whether the exercises we did at home were even helping.

One of us had insurance that covered 12 weeks of PT. The other had to stop after four weeks because it was $150 per session out-of-pocket. We both got the same surgery. But our recoveries looked completely different‚Äînot because of the injury, but because of cost and access.

That felt wrong. Recovery shouldn't depend on whether you can afford to keep showing up to a clinic twice a week. So we started asking: what if there was a way to do guided rehab at home, with real feedback, for a fraction of the cost?

That question became Regenix. A wearable device that assists your knee during exercises and tracks whether you're getting stronger. It's not a replacement for physical therapy‚Äîit's a tool to extend what you can do between sessions, or after insurance stops covering visits.

What We're Building Toward

A device that costs less than two PT sessions but can be used for months. Light enough to wear at home. Smart enough to know if exercises are being done correctly.

Target: under $300, under 3 kg, works without an internet connection.

Where We Want This to Go

A world where recovering from a knee injury doesn't require driving to a clinic twice a week for three months‚Äîor skipping rehab because it's too expensive.

Fewer missed sessions. Better adherence. More consistent outcomes.

What Guides Our Decisions

Keep it affordable

We chose cable-driven actuation over motors because it's lighter and costs 80% less. We use smartphone-grade sensors instead of lab equipment. Every design choice is filtered through the question: "Does this make it cheaper without making it worse?"

Design for real bodies

Rigid exoskeletons hurt when your knee doesn't align perfectly with the hinge. We built a five-bar linkage that follows your knee's natural motion instead of forcing it into a fixed path. It took six prototypes to get the geometry right.

Reduce barriers to access

Regenix works offline after initial setup. It doesn't require a subscription. The battery lasts a week. These aren't features‚Äîthey're requirements if this is going to work in places where clinics are two hours away or internet is unreliable.

Build something that works

We're not interested in flashy demos. The device has to survive 30-minute sessions, give accurate feedback, and actually help people get stronger. We test every claim with users. If it doesn't work, we change it.

What a Rehab Session Looks Like

A realistic walkthrough of a typical 30-minute session at home

0:00

Put on the device

Strap the thigh cuff first, then the calf cuff. Tighten until snug but not restrictive. The device should feel secure when you stand up. Takes about 90 seconds once you're used to it.

1:30

Calibration check

The device measures your neutral knee position and maximum comfortable range of motion. Bend and straighten your leg a few times. A small vibration confirms calibration is complete.

3:00

Warm-up reps

Start with assisted range-of-motion exercises. Sit on a chair and extend your leg slowly. The device provides light assistance at the end of the movement to help you reach full extension without straining.

8:00

Strengthening exercises

Move to standing exercises like partial squats or step-ups. The device monitors how much force you're generating and how consistent your form is. If fatigue is detected, assistance increases slightly to maintain range of motion.

18:00

Fatigue detected

The device notices your reps are slowing down and you're relying more on assistance. A gentle buzz suggests taking a 60-second break. This prevents you from compensating with bad form.

25:00

Cool-down and summary

Finish with gentle flexion exercises. The device displays a summary: reps completed, range of motion achieved, assistance level compared to last session. Data is saved locally.

28:00

Remove device

Loosen straps and slide off. Total session time: about 30 minutes. The device charges via USB-C and lasts about a week between charges.

After the Session

Progress tracking: The device shows whether your range of motion increased, whether you needed less assistance, and whether you maintained good form throughout.

No internet required: All data is stored on the device. You can sync to a phone app later to see trends over weeks, but it's not required for the device to work.

Recommended frequency: 3-4 sessions per week. The device will remind you if more than 3 days pass without a session, but it won't nag you.

Who Regenix Is Not For

Being clear about what this device doesn't do

Complete paralysis or neurological impairment

Regenix provides assistive force, but it doesn't move your leg for you. If you have no voluntary muscle control in your leg due to spinal cord injury or stroke, this device won't help. It's designed for people who can initiate movement but need help completing it.

Acute injuries or unstable joints

If you just injured your knee (within the first 48-72 hours) or your joint is unstable, you shouldn't be doing active rehab yet‚Äîwith or without Regenix. This device is for the rehabilitation phase, not the acute injury phase. Always get medical clearance before starting any rehab protocol.

Permanent mobility replacement

Regenix is a rehab tool, not a permanent assistive device. It's meant to be used during recovery, not worn all day or used indefinitely. If you need long-term mobility support, you need a different solution‚Äîlikely a custom orthosis or mobility aid prescribed by a specialist.

Complex multi-joint coordination

This device only addresses the knee. If your injury or condition affects your hip, ankle, or requires full-leg coordination (e.g., gait retraining after a femur fracture), Regenix won't provide comprehensive support. It's a single-joint rehab tool.

Substitute for professional diagnosis

The device tracks progress, but it doesn't diagnose problems or replace a physical therapist's assessment. If you're experiencing new pain, swelling, or regression, you need to see a healthcare provider. Regenix is a tool to support a rehab plan, not create one.

If you're unsure whether Regenix is right for your situation, talk to your physical therapist or orthopedic doctor. We'd rather you get the right tool than try to fit into the wrong one.
