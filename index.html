How Regenix Works

A semi-rigid exoskeleton that moves with your knee, not against it

The device attaches to your thigh and calf with adjustable straps. The linkage follows your knee's natural arc during movement.

What Happens Mechanically

When you bend or straighten your knee, a five-bar linkage system moves with you. Unlike a rigid brace with a single hinge, this linkage tracks the combined rolling and sliding motion your knee naturally does.

A cable system connected to the linkage can pull gently to assist extension (straightening) or flexion (bending). The assistance is controlled by a small motor, but most of the work is still done by your muscles. Think of it like training wheels—support when you need it, not a replacement for pedaling.

What Happens Digitally

Sensors measure three things: knee angle, movement speed, and the force being applied by your leg. Every time you do a repetition, the device records whether you completed the full range of motion and how much assistance was needed.

An onboard algorithm compares today's session to previous ones. If you're completing reps with less assistance or moving through a wider range, the device flags that as progress. If fatigue is detected (slower reps, more assistance needed), it suggests stopping before form breaks down.

What You Feel

At first, the device feels snug but not restrictive. When you start an exercise, it moves smoothly with your leg—there's no fighting against a rigid hinge.

When your muscles start to tire, you'll feel a gentle pull helping you complete the movement. It's not dramatic—more like someone placing a hand under your knee and giving a small boost. As you get stronger, you'll notice the device assisting less over time.

The Key Components

Five-bar linkage

Follows the knee's natural instant center of rotation. Eliminates the pressure points caused by single-axis hinges.

Cable-driven assist

Lightweight tensile cables provide smooth, variable assistance. Motor activates only when needed based on real-time force sensing.

IMU + force sensors

Inertial measurement units track position and velocity. Force sensor measures leg output to calculate fatigue.

Embedded algorithm

Runs locally on the device. Compares current performance to baseline. Flags progress or regression in real time.

Four Adaptive Training Modes

Regenix adapts to different recovery phases with intelligent assistance that responds to patient effort

Assistive Training

Provides continuous motor support to help patients initiate and complete movements when strength is limited.

The motor actively assists throughout the entire range of motion, making it ideal for early-stage recovery when patients struggle to complete basic movements on their own.

Protective Training

Requires active user effort—the motor only engages once the patient applies a minimum force threshold.

This ensures assistance is given only when the user is genuinely attempting the movement, preventing passive reliance and encouraging active muscle engagement.

Resistive Training

Scales resistance based on the patient's own force input: the harder they push, the more resistance the system applies.

This enables controlled strength development by automatically adjusting difficulty to match the user's current output, creating an adaptive challenge.

Testing Mode

Runs standardized movements to measure force and joint angle, generating objective data to track progress.

These measurements provide quantifiable metrics that help personalize training protocols and identify when patients are ready to advance to more challenging exercises.

Progressive Recovery Path

Patients typically start with Assistive Training when strength is lowest, progress to Protective Training as they regain voluntary control, then advance to Resistive Training for strength building. Testing Mode is used periodically throughout to objectively measure progress and inform when to increase difficulty.

Meet RENA

Regenerative Exoskeleton Network Assistant—the AI that guides your recovery

What RENA Does

RENA is the embedded software that runs on the Regenix device. It processes sensor data in real time, adjusts training modes based on your performance, and provides verbal or visual cues to guide you through exercises.

During a session, RENA monitors your knee angle, movement velocity, and force output. If you're struggling to complete a rep, it switches from Protective to Assistive mode. If you're breezing through sets, it increases resistance automatically.

RENA also tracks fatigue patterns. If your movement speed drops or you start compensating with your hip instead of your knee, it suggests taking a break or ending the session early to prevent injury.

Real-Time Feedback

Simple audio cues like "good rep," "increase range," or "slow down" help you adjust form without staring at a screen. Visual progress bars show how close you are to completing a set.

Adaptive Progression

RENA compares your current session to previous ones and automatically adjusts difficulty. If you've hit the same performance metrics three sessions in a row, it suggests increasing resistance or range of motion.

Safety Guardrails

RENA has built-in limits based on clinical protocols. It won't let you progress to Resistive Training until you've demonstrated consistent performance in Protective mode. It stops sessions if it detects compensation patterns that could cause secondary injuries.

Not a Replacement for Clinical Judgment

RENA handles the repetitive work—counting reps, monitoring form, adjusting resistance—but it doesn't replace a physical therapist's expertise. Complex decisions about when to progress to new exercises, how to address pain, or when surgical intervention is needed still require human clinical judgment.

Think of RENA as the assistant that keeps you on track between PT visits, not the one making diagnostic decisions.
